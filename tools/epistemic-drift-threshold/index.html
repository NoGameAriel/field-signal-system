When Learning Stops Working

How to recognize the earliest warning sign that a system has been captured—before the evidence shows up anywhere else.

```

There's a specific moment when a system stops being fair and starts being something worse.

It doesn't happen all at once. It happens when you notice that no matter how much you pay attention—no matter how carefully you track what's working, adjust your approach, try harder—your understanding of the system stops improving. You're not learning anything. You're just cycling.

This is the earliest warning sign we know of. Earlier than financial data. Earlier than policy changes. Earlier than anyone in charge will admit something is wrong.

At the moment when learning stops updating reality, capture has already begun.

How to recognize it

The Signals

You've probably felt this before. It shows up as:

  • "It used to make sense and now it doesn't—but I can't point to what changed."
  • Putting in more effort and getting less predictable results.
  • Hearing yourself explain outcomes with words like "the algorithm," "timing," or "luck"—because nothing more specific holds up.
  • Watching people around you stop asking why things work and start just chasing what works right now.
  • A quiet exhaustion that comes not from the work itself but from trying to understand a system that keeps not making sense.

That exhaustion is a signal. It's your intelligence telling you the feedback has broken.

How systems degrade

The Signal Continuity Lifecycle

Systems don't collapse overnight. They slide. Here is what that slide looks like from the inside.

Stage 1 Direct Continuity Healthy

The system is legible. Effort produces understanding. You can trace where value comes from and why outcomes happen. Your mental model of how it works actually matches reality.

Example: A customer pays a farmer directly. Both parties can see exactly what they're exchanging and why.

Stage 2 Compressed Continuity Healthy

The system has scaled. Things are more abstracted, but still traceable. You might not see every detail, but you can still understand how it works in broad strokes. Accountability exists, even if it's shared.

Example: A cooperative or union pools contributions—individual lines blur, but the overall logic remains visible and reversible.

Stage 3 Abstracted Continuity Fragile

The system still functions—but it's becoming harder to learn. Your model of how it works starts to drift from what actually happens. Effort and outcome are decoupling. You can feel something has changed but can't name it.

This is where most systems that feel unfair are actually living. Not broken—but becoming unlearnable.

Example: A social media creator notices their content is performing differently, but the platform won't explain why. They start optimizing for signals they don't understand.

Stage 3 → 4 The Threshold ⚑ EDT Fires Here

This is the moment the Epistemic Drift Threshold detects. Adaptation stops updating your internal model. You try things, some work, but you can't build cumulative understanding from the results. The system has stopped being a learnable environment.

This is earlier than any financial signal. Earlier than any policy document. It shows up in human experience first.

Stage 4 Opaque Continuity Pre-Capture

The origin of value technically exists somewhere in the system—but it's no longer operationally visible. Redistribution happens through layers you can't access. Consent has become assumed rather than revisited. The system is no longer contestable even if data still exists.

Example: Gig platforms, ad tech ecosystems. You can see outputs. You cannot see the logic producing them.

Stage 5 Signal Severance Extraction

Origin has been functionally detached from outcome. Value flows without readable lineage. You cannot reconstruct how reward connected to contribution. Extraction has become structurally normalized—not a bug, but the operating logic.

Example: Black-box ranking systems, opaque financial derivatives, hidden fee structures where the logic of value movement cannot be recovered.

Why this matters

Most ways of detecting capture look for evidence after the damage is done—a rule changed, money moved, someone got hurt. This detects it earlier: at the moment when the system stops being something you can understand through honest engagement with it. That's when the structure has already shifted, even if nothing looks different yet.

The diagnostic question

Ask Yourself

Practitioner Prompt
Is my effort still teaching me something—or am I just running strategies that sometimes work for reasons I can't track?

If it's the second one, the system has already moved past the point where it can be navigated honestly. That's not a personal failure. That's a structural signal.
```
